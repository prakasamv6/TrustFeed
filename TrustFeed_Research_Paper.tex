\begin{abstract}
Generative AI has made synthetic text, images, and video ordinary components of social feeds, while platform responses remain fragmented across weak labels, hidden metadata, and brittle detector-centric interventions. This creates a human-centered governance problem: users need origin cues, platforms need contestable workflows, and researchers need ways to inspect how algorithmic judgments vary across perspectives rather than treating a single model output as authoritative. This paper presents \textbf{TrustFeed}, a deployed two-application prototype for AI-origin disclosure, community contestation, and authenticity observability.

TrustFeed's central technical contribution is a \textbf{baseline-anchored bias observability architecture}. Instead of treating multiple agent outputs as votes toward a final truth label, TrustFeed compares several perspective-conditioned agents against a non-bias baseline agent, then computes disagreement, bias delta, dominance, and debiased scores with factor-level attribution. This design makes model disagreement inspectable and contestable at the feed interface. Around that observability core, the system implements three governance mechanisms: (1) default AI-origin disclosure in the main post viewport, (2) public notification and community verification for contested content, and (3) trend-level circuit breakers that slow amplification when synthetic ratio, coordination, and trust signals exceed configured thresholds. The prototype comprises a core social-feed platform (Angular front end, FastAPI/Python backend) and a companion survey application (Angular/Express/MySQL) for human evaluation without storing personally identifiable information.

The paper makes four contributions. First, it reframes AI disclosure as a platform interface primitive rather than a buried compliance feature. Second, it introduces baseline-anchored bias observability as a governance-oriented alternative to black-box ensemble scoring. Third, it describes a working artifact that integrates disclosure, contestation, multimodal analysis, dashboard observability, and survey instrumentation into one research platform. Fourth, it clarifies an evidence model that distinguishes implemented capabilities from forward-looking research extensions. TrustFeed is therefore positioned not as a universal AI detector, but as a prototype-backed socio-technical framework for studying how disclosure, contestation, and observability can preserve human judgment under pervasive synthetic media.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Synthetic media is now part of ordinary platform use. Text, images, audio, and video generated wholly or partly by machine learning systems appear in social feeds, messaging threads, and recommender surfaces at a scale that exceeds ordinary users' ability to identify them reliably \cite{lazer2018sciencefake, vosoughi2018spread, tolosana2020deepfakes}. The platform question is therefore no longer whether synthetic content exists, but how social systems should communicate origin, uncertainty, and contestation once human-authored and machine-generated content are interleaved in the same feed.

Current responses remain incomplete. Some systems rely on fragile watermarking or media forensics \cite{kirchenbauer2023watermark}. Some provide labels whose effects depend heavily on wording, placement, and information density \cite{aslett2022labels, li2024aigclabels, wittenberg2025labeling, gamage2025labeling}. Provenance initiatives such as C2PA and Content Credentials provide an important substrate, but the existence of machine-readable provenance does not by itself solve the interface problem of when, where, and how to surface origin cues in a platform experience \cite{c2pa2025spec, cai2026howitworks}. At the same time, recent work suggests that repeated exposure to AI outputs can alter human perceptual, emotional, and social judgments, raising the stakes for feed-level governance rather than item-level labeling alone \cite{glickman2025feedback}.

This paper argues for a stronger design position: \textbf{AI-origin disclosure should be a platform interface primitive, and authenticity governance should be built around contestable observability rather than opaque detector outputs.} In practical terms, users should not have to hunt for metadata in order to know how content was made, and platforms should not present a single model score as if it were the last word on authenticity. Instead, platforms should expose origin claims, community disagreement, and model disagreement in ways that are legible, actionable, and auditable.

We present \textbf{TrustFeed}, a deployed two-application prototype that operationalizes that thesis. TrustFeed Core is a social-feed application that surfaces author-side AI declaration, post-level AI badges, community verification controls, transparency panels, bias observability dashboards, and trend-level circuit breakers. TrustFeed Survey is a companion application that records human judgments, confidence ratings, and agent outputs in a privacy-preserving evaluation workflow. The prototype integrates multimodal analyzers, a six-signal AI-content scoring engine, public notification, topic-level safeguards, and a dashboard layer for feed observability.

The manuscript's central contribution is not merely that TrustFeed is implemented. The central novelty is a \textbf{baseline-anchored bias observability architecture}. Rather than interpreting multiple agent outputs as a democratic vote over truth, TrustFeed uses one non-bias baseline agent as a stable reference and compares it to several \emph{perspective agents} configured to stress-test how authenticity scoring changes under different feature sensitivities. The system then exposes bias delta, disagreement, dominance, debiased score, and factor attribution to the interface. This makes disagreement inspectable and makes bias itself a first-class governance signal.

The paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item It reframes AI-origin disclosure from a buried compliance feature into a platform interface primitive.
    \item It introduces baseline-anchored bias observability as a governance-oriented alternative to black-box ensemble scoring.
    \item It describes a working two-application artifact that integrates disclosure, contestation, multimodal analysis, dashboard observability, and survey instrumentation into one research platform.
    \item It clarifies an evidence model by distinguishing implemented capabilities from forward-looking research extensions, especially for exposure-aware circuit breakers.
\end{enumerate}

This framing intentionally narrows the claim surface. TrustFeed is not presented as a universal AI detector or a completed causal evaluation. It is a prototype-backed socio-technical framework for studying how disclosure, contestation, and observability can preserve human judgment under conditions of pervasive synthetic media.

\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{Synthetic media, misinformation, and labeling}

Misinformation research has established that false content can spread farther and faster than true content online, particularly when novelty and frictionless sharing are rewarded by platform dynamics \cite{lazer2018sciencefake, vosoughi2018spread}. Generative AI increases the scale and plausibility of that problem by lowering the cost of producing persuasive and personalized content \cite{goldstein2023generative}. At the same time, human performance in deepfake detection is uneven and context-sensitive \cite{tolosana2020deepfakes, somoray2025deepfake}, which weakens strategies that rely solely on end-user vigilance.

Research on labels and warnings offers a mixed picture. Visible labels can help users identify AI-generated content, but label presence alone often does not strongly reduce engagement or misperception \cite{aslett2022labels, li2024aigclabels, wittenberg2025labeling}. Label design also matters substantially: iconography, information density, and wording influence whether users understand and trust the signal \cite{gamage2025labeling}. These findings motivate disclosure systems that are not only visible, but embedded in a broader governance workflow.

\subsection{Provenance, contestability, and community verification}

Content provenance initiatives such as C2PA and Content Credentials provide structured ways to bind origin information to media assets \cite{c2pa2025spec, cai2026howitworks}. Policy developments, including the EU AI Act and subsequent guidance on marking and labeling AI-generated content, further reinforce disclosure as a normative direction \cite{euai2024, eucommission2026}. But provenance data has limited value if it remains buried in secondary menus or specialized tooling. A human-centered platform must surface provenance in an interface that ordinary users can act upon.

Research on community-based fact-checking suggests that public contestation can play a constructive role. Community Notes-style interventions can increase trust in corrective context and can reduce engagement with false information in some settings \cite{drolsbach2024community, slaughter2025community}. More broadly, human-AI interaction guidelines emphasize calibration, clear explanations, and reversible actions \cite{amershi2019guidelines}. Taken together, these works support authenticity systems that combine machine judgment, user declaration, public notification, and revisable state rather than relying on one hidden model output.

\subsection{Why observability matters}

Most authenticity systems still optimize for classification accuracy or detector performance. TrustFeed instead starts from a governance question: what must users and moderators be able to \emph{see} in order to reason well under uncertainty? This paper treats observability as the missing connective tissue between disclosure and action. The goal is not to produce one authoritative authenticity score, but to make disagreement, bias, and contestation visible in a way that supports better downstream judgment. This observability stance also aligns with evidence that human-AI feedback loops can amplify judgment drift over repeated exposure \cite{glickman2025feedback, spearing2025countering}.

\section{Design Thesis}

TrustFeed is built on three claims.

\textbf{First,} origin information is a material property of public digital content. Users should not need forensic expertise to know whether the expressive core of a post was AI-generated, AI-assisted, human-declared, unavailable, or disputed.

\textbf{Second,} authenticity is not only a detection problem; it is a contestable governance problem. Creator declarations, public notifications, provenance cues, and model outputs can disagree, and platforms should surface that disagreement rather than hiding it behind a single confidence score.

\textbf{Third,} the right unit of intervention is often the \emph{feed}, not the isolated item. If synthetic or disputed content accumulates in a stream, the resulting exposure regime can matter as much as any individual post. This motivates circuit-breaker logic as a platform safeguard, even if the stronger user-level exposure model remains future work.

These claims yield four system requirements:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Disclosure by default:} every public post should surface an origin state in the primary viewport.
    \item \textbf{Contestability by design:} viewers must be able to notify or dispute origin claims.
    \item \textbf{Observability, not opacity:} model disagreement and bias should be inspectable rather than hidden.
    \item \textbf{Feed-level safeguards:} platforms need mechanisms to slow amplification when risky synthetic concentration emerges.
\end{enumerate}

\section{System Overview}

TrustFeed is realized as two deployed applications on DigitalOcean App Platform:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{TrustFeed Core}, a social-feed platform built with Angular and a FastAPI/Python backend.
    \item \textbf{TrustFeed Survey}, a companion evaluation application built with Angular, Express.js, and MySQL.
\end{itemize}

TrustFeed Core supports author-side AI declaration, inline origin badges, confidence states, community voting, transparency panels, bias dashboards, trend monitoring, and corrective actions. TrustFeed Survey presents content to human participants, captures authenticity judgments and confidence, and records corresponding agent outputs without storing personally identifiable information.

The system integrates multimodal analysis for text, image, and video, a six-signal AI-content scoring engine, dashboard endpoints for descriptive observability, and trend-level circuit breakers for suspicious topic escalation. The survey application provides a path toward controlled comparative evaluation without conflating architecture with already-established causal performance.

\section{Baseline-Anchored Bias Observability}

\subsection{Why this is the paper's distinctive contribution}

The paper's strongest differentiator is its bias observability architecture. Most multi-model or multi-agent systems either average outputs or stage them as a majority vote. That approach may hide systematic disagreement and make it harder to understand whether variance is caused by model sensitivity, proxy features, or strategic weighting. TrustFeed instead makes disagreement visible by design.

The system runs several \emph{perspective agents} alongside one \emph{non-bias baseline agent}. The perspective agents are not presented as true regional cognition models. They are better understood as parameterized stress-test lenses that vary feature sensitivity and directional bias so that the system can inspect how authenticity scoring changes under different assumptions. This reframing is deliberate: it avoids overstating what the agents represent while preserving their utility as governance instruments.

\subsection{Architecture}

The backend dispatches content to six parallel agents: five perspective-conditioned agents and one non-bias baseline. The baseline passes through the raw model output without perspective-specific adjustment. Each perspective agent modifies the score using feature weights, text sensitivities, and directional intensity terms. The baseline then acts as the reference point for all downstream observability metrics.

For each item, TrustFeed computes:
\begin{align}
\text{biasDelta} &= |s_{\text{raw}} - s_{\text{baseline}}| \\
\text{debiased} &= s_{\text{baseline}} + \text{clamp}(s_{\text{raw}} - s_{\text{baseline}}, -r_{\max}, r_{\max}) \\
\text{BAI} &= s_{\text{raw}} / s_{\text{baseline}} \\
\text{disagreement} &= \sigma_p(s_1, \ldots, s_5, s_{\text{baseline}}) \\
\text{dominance} &= \max(s_i) - \bar{s}_{\text{perspective}}
\end{align}
where $r_{\max}=0.05$ caps residual adjustment and $\sigma_p$ is the population standard deviation across all agent scores.

The system further decomposes divergence into factor-level attributions, allowing the interface to show which features most strongly influenced the deviation from baseline. This makes bias itself observable, not merely inferred after the fact.

\subsection{Why the baseline matters}

The non-bias baseline agent plays three roles:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Calibration anchor:} it provides a reference against which perspective-conditioned inflation or deflation can be bounded.
    \item \textbf{Audit anchor:} it lets the system classify disagreement into interpretable categories rather than treating variance as random noise.
    \item \textbf{Explanation anchor:} it enables factor attribution by tying divergence to specific features rather than leaving disagreement opaque.
\end{enumerate}

This is the paper's core novelty claim. TrustFeed is not distinctive simply because it uses multiple agents. It is unique because it uses a baseline-anchored observability model to make disagreement explicit, attributable, and contestable.

\section{Interface and Interaction Design}

\subsection{Default disclosure states}

TrustFeed uses a disclosure taxonomy that distinguishes creation process rather than collapsing content into a single binary verdict. The main states are \emph{AI-generated}, \emph{AI-assisted}, \emph{Human-declared}, \emph{Provenance unavailable}, and \emph{Disputed}. This taxonomy is closer to origin labeling than to moderation labeling: it communicates how content came into being without itself asserting falsity or policy violation.

\subsection{Contestability workflow}

Each post supports community verification actions. Viewers can vote that a post appears AI-generated, human-authored, disputed, or misleading. Those inputs update post-level summaries and contribute to the community signal used by the trust analysis endpoint. Uploaders can revise their own declaration, and moderator or provenance evidence can revise the visible state. The interface therefore treats authenticity as a revisable condition rather than a terminal classification.

\subsection{Transparency and dashboard observability}

TrustFeed's interface includes a transparency panel and dashboard layer. At the post level, users can inspect agent scores, factor attributions, and corrective-action recommendations. At the feed level, dashboards summarize total analyses, bias delta, flagged count, and rolling trends. These dashboards are not presented as proof of detector accuracy. Their role is to make the state of the feed inspectable to users and researchers.

\section{Implementation Summary}

The implementation is intentionally summarized rather than exhaustively catalogued in the main text.

\textbf{Front end.} TrustFeed Core is an Angular single-page application with standalone components for feed rendering, post cards, post creation, review panels, transparency views, dashboards, and accessibility controls. TrustFeed Survey is a separate Angular interface for structured human evaluation.

\textbf{Back end.} The Core API is implemented in FastAPI/Python and exposes endpoints for analysis, detection, trending, community flagging, and dashboards. The survey API is implemented in Express.js with MySQL persistence.

\textbf{Analysis pipeline.} The backend orchestrates an eight-node LangGraph DAG that prepares content, fans out to agents, aggregates scores, debiases against the baseline, performs bias detection, attributes factors, generates explanation text, and builds a downloadable report.

\textbf{Content analyzers.} The prototype supports text, image, and video. Text analysis uses RoBERTa-derived features; image analysis uses OpenCV and Vision Transformer features; video analysis aggregates frame-level signals.

\textbf{AI-content scoring.} TrustFeed implements a six-signal weighted engine that combines linguistic, structural, statistical, community, author declaration, and multi-agent signals into an AI-likelihood estimate and a corresponding trust score.

\textbf{Platform safeguards.} The system includes a fully implemented trend-level circuit breaker that monitors topic velocity, AI-content ratio, coordination, author diversity, and composite trust score.

This level of detail is sufficient to understand the system while keeping the manuscript focused on the paper's distinct contribution.

\section{Trend-Level Circuit Breakers}

The manuscript distinguishes clearly between implemented and proposed circuit-breaker mechanisms.

\subsection{Implemented: trend-level topic breaker}

TrustFeed currently implements a \textbf{topic-level} circuit breaker. For each trending topic, the backend monitors five signals: posting velocity, AI-content ratio, coordination score, author diversity, and trust score. When configured thresholds are crossed, the system can suppress the topic from the trending feed, add an under-review state, throttle engagement, and notify moderators.

This feature is implemented and constitutes the systems contribution at the circuit-breaker layer.

\subsection{Proposed: user-level exposure-aware breaker}

The stronger \textbf{user-level} exposure model remains future work. In that model, circuit-breaker risk is defined over a rolling window of impressions:
\begin{align}
A(u,w) &= \frac{\text{AI-labeled impressions}}{\text{total impressions}} \\
D(u,w) &= \frac{\text{disputed impressions}}{\text{total impressions}} \\
V(u,w) &= 1 - \frac{H(\text{source categories})}{\log K} \\
J(u,w) &= |\hat{p}_{u,w} - b_u| \\
P(u,w) &= \frac{\text{public-interest synthetic impressions}}{\text{total impressions}}
\end{align}
with
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CBR}(u,w) = \alpha A(u,w) + \beta D(u,w) + \gamma V(u,w) + \delta J(u,w) + \epsilon P(u,w).
\end{equation}

This formal model is retained because it is conceptually important, but it is treated explicitly as a research extension rather than a completed deployment claim.

\section{Evidence Model and Evaluation Strategy}

The paper's main methodological challenge is evidence mismatch, so the evidence model is made explicit here.

\subsection{What the paper currently demonstrates}

The manuscript can currently support four kinds of evidence:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Artifact evidence:} the system exists as two deployed applications with implemented disclosure, contestation, dashboard, and trend-breaker features.
    \item \textbf{Architectural evidence:} the backend pipeline, scoring logic, and survey instrumentation are concrete rather than speculative.
    \item \textbf{Observability evidence:} dashboards and reports expose descriptive platform state, including disagreement and bias metrics.
    \item \textbf{Methodological evidence:} the survey application provides a ready pathway for controlled comparative evaluation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What the paper does not yet claim}

The paper does \emph{not} claim completed causal proof that TrustFeed improves authenticity judgment, reduces misinformation, or eliminates harmful bias. It also does not claim that the perspective agents model real geographic cognition or that the AI-content detector provides ground-truth authorship attribution in all contexts.

\subsection{Conference-appropriate evaluation agenda}

A strong next-step evaluation would test four conditions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item no label,
    \item disclosure only,
    \item disclosure plus contestation,
    \item disclosure plus contestation plus circuit-breaker friction.
\end{enumerate}
The primary outcome would be authenticity judgment accuracy; secondary outcomes would include trust calibration, sharing restraint, perceived agency, and appeal fairness.

This agenda is retained because it shows that the paper is ready for empirical follow-through, but the manuscript now clearly treats it as an evaluation plan rather than a disguised results section.

\section{Governance and Safeguards}

TrustFeed is not only an interface artifact. It also encodes governance obligations.

\textbf{Privacy and minimization.} The survey application stores no personally identifiable information, and exposure-related signals are framed as retention-bounded, purpose-specific measures.

\textbf{Appeals and revisions.} Any disputed origin state requires an explanation and a revision path, otherwise contestability becomes unilateral.

\textbf{Abuse resistance.} Public notification is vulnerable to brigading and coordinated gaming; rate limiting, aggregation, and anomaly monitoring are therefore part of the design.

\textbf{Accessibility.} Disclosure states should not be conveyed by color alone. Iconography, wording, and accessible controls remain part of the artifact.

\textbf{Calibration drift.} Thresholds and weighting rules cannot be assumed stable as synthetic media practices evolve.

These safeguards matter because authenticity governance requires institutional discipline, not only technical novelty.

\section{Limitations}

TrustFeed should be interpreted as a \emph{prototype-backed human-centered authenticity governance system}, not as a production-ready provenance platform, a universal AI detector, or a completed intervention study.

First, the present work is strongest as an interface, workflow, and observability contribution. It shows how origin signals, public contestation, and model disagreement can be surfaced in a social-feed context, but it does not yet implement a full end-to-end provenance enforcement stack with cross-platform interoperability.

Second, the system's visible states -- including \emph{Likely AI}, \emph{Likely Human}, and \emph{Disputed} -- are decision-support cues under uncertainty, not forensic verdicts. Creator declarations may be inaccurate, community signals may be noisy, and provenance may be absent.

Third, the perspective agents should not be overinterpreted as representations of real regional cognition. In this paper they are more defensibly understood as \emph{stress-test lenses} used to expose scoring sensitivity relative to a non-bias baseline.

Fourth, dashboard outputs are descriptive observability measures, not causal evidence that the system improves epistemic outcomes. Stronger behavioral claims still require controlled evaluation.

Fifth, the user-level exposure-aware circuit breaker remains a formalized research proposal rather than a fully validated intervention mechanism.

Finally, TrustFeed should not be framed as anti-AI. Its contribution is to make origin more visible, disagreement more actionable, and feed conditions more governable in environments where synthetic and human-authored content increasingly coexist.

\section{Conclusion}

The synthetic-media era requires more than weak labels and hidden provenance metadata. It requires platform designs that expose origin, support contestation, and make model disagreement inspectable where users actually make decisions. TrustFeed advances this agenda through a deployed two-application prototype for disclosure, community verification, dashboard observability, and trend-level safeguards.

The paper's central contribution is \textbf{baseline-anchored bias observability}: a design in which a non-bias baseline agent anchors multiple perspective-conditioned agents so that disagreement, bias delta, debiased score, and factor attribution become visible governance signals rather than hidden model internals. That contribution gives the paper a clearer and more distinctive identity for an international conference audience. It shifts the story from ``we built many components'' to ``we offer a new way to govern authenticity under uncertainty.''

TrustFeed is therefore best understood as a prototype-backed socio-technical artifact: implemented enough to study, disciplined enough not to overclaim, and distinctive enough to contribute a novel observability perspective to the growing literature on AI-origin disclosure and social-platform governance.
