\begin{abstract}

Generative models are moving synthetic text, images, audio, and video into ordinary social feeds faster than users can reliably detect them. Yet the dominant platform response still treats AI disclosure as optional, sporadic, weakly visible, or disconnected from downstream user action. This paper argues that AI-origin disclosure should become a platform primitive, analogous to origin and ingredient labeling in food systems: informative rather than moralizing, process-focused rather than quality-certifying, and visible by default rather than buried in metadata. We present TrustFeed, a human-centered architecture and proof-of-concept platform that combines (1) default visible disclosure of AI-generated or AI-assisted content, (2) public notification and community verification when users believe a post is synthetic, and (3) an exposure-aware circuit breaker that intervenes when synthetic saturation, dispute, or judgment drift exceeds a safe threshold. Grounded in a repository-based Angular prototype with creator disclosure, visual AI badges, community voting, confidence indicators, filtering, and platform statistics, the paper extends the artifact into a broader governance methodology integrating provenance signals, contestability, and friction-based interventions. Drawing on legacy and recent work on misinformation diffusion, deepfake detection, provenance standards, community fact-checking, human-AI feedback loops, and synthetic-media labeling, we synthesize a design framework for platform accountability. The paper contributes a disclosure taxonomy, a formal circuit-breaker risk model, and an empirical evaluation protocol comparing no-label, disclosure-only, disclosure-plus-public-notification, and full circuit-breaker conditions. TrustFeed is positioned not as a binary AI detector, but as a platform-safety architecture for preserving human judgment under conditions of pervasive synthetic media.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Synthetic media is no longer exceptional. Text, images, audio, and video produced wholly or partly by generative models are now embedded in everyday social browsing, recommendation systems, and messaging environments. At the same time, classic misinformation research has shown that false content spreads farther and faster than true content online, particularly in politically salient contexts \cite{lazer2018sciencefake, vosoughi2018spread}. Generative AI increases the scale, speed, and aesthetic plausibility of this problem by sharply lowering the cost of producing persuasive, personalized, and visually convincing content \cite{goldstein2023generative, tolosana2020deepfakes}. 

The central policy and design question is therefore no longer whether platforms should respond to synthetic content, but \emph{how}. Current responses remain fragmented. Some systems rely on fragile media forensics or watermarking \cite{kirchenbauer2023watermark}; some apply limited labels whose effects vary with wording and placement \cite{aslett2022labels, li2024aigclabels, wittenberg2025labeling}; some provenance initiatives expose rich metadata, but only when platforms surface it effectively \cite{c2pa2025spec, cai2026howitworks}. Meanwhile, human users are poor and highly variable detectors of deepfakes and other synthetic media, especially in ecologically realistic settings \cite{somoray2025deepfake}. Label-only interventions can help identify that something is AI-generated, but often do not strongly reduce credibility beliefs or engagement by themselves \cite{aslett2022labels, li2024aigclabels, wittenberg2025labeling}. 

This paper advances a stronger thesis: \textbf{AI-origin disclosure should be the default interaction contract for synthetic content on public digital platforms.} In the same way that food systems distinguish between categories such as organic and non-organic as a matter of disclosure rather than taste, social platforms should visibly indicate whether content is AI-generated, AI-assisted, human-declared, or provenance-unavailable. This is not a claim that AI-generated content is inherently lower quality, deceptive, or prohibited. It is a claim that the production process is a material fact users deserve to know before they decide whether to believe, share, or rely on content.

Default disclosure alone, however, is insufficient. Research on human--AI feedback loops shows that repeated interaction with AI outputs can alter and amplify human perceptual, emotional, and social judgments \cite{glickman2025feedback}. Research on community fact-checking suggests that crowd annotation can increase trust in corrective context and, in some settings, reduce the diffusion of false information \cite{drolsbach2024community, slaughter2025community}. These findings motivate a broader architecture in which disclosure is paired with contestability, community input, and friction-based safety interventions. 

We therefore present \textbf{TrustFeed}, a human-centered authenticity governance framework grounded in an existing Angular proof-of-concept repository. The current artifact already supports creator-side AI declaration, visible AI badges, community voting, confidence labels, filtering, and summary metrics \cite{trustfeedrepo}. We extend that artifact conceptually into a full platform methodology with three pillars:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Default AI-origin disclosure}: the platform surfaces an origin label for every item by default, rather than only when users search for metadata.
    \item \textbf{Public notification and community verification}: users can notify or contest whether content is AI-generated, and the platform aggregates those signals into a revisable confidence state.
    \item \textbf{Circuit breakers}: the platform monitors synthetic-content saturation, dispute, and judgment drift and applies progressive friction when exposure becomes risky.
\end{enumerate}

The contributions of this paper are fourfold. First, it reframes AI disclosure from a compliance afterthought into a platform primitive. Second, it synthesizes provenance, community notification, and circuit-breaking into one human-centered architecture. Third, it formalizes an exposure-aware circuit-breaker methodology for social platforms. Fourth, it proposes an evaluation protocol suitable for testing whether these interventions improve authenticity judgments, trust calibration, and sharing restraint relative to simpler label-only baselines.

\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{Misinformation diffusion and synthetic media}

Legacy misinformation research established the structural conditions that make platform-scale deception dangerous. Lazer et al.\ argued that the contemporary information ecosystem rewards novelty, virality, and low-friction sharing, thereby creating fertile conditions for fake news \cite{lazer2018sciencefake}. Vosoughi et al.\ found that false news diffused farther, faster, deeper, and more broadly than true news on Twitter, with especially strong effects in political content \cite{vosoughi2018spread}. These findings matter because generative AI can now mass-produce the kinds of emotionally engaging and novel-looking artifacts that exploit these dynamics \cite{goldstein2023generative}. 

Deepfake research further shows that synthetic audiovisual media is increasingly difficult to detect reliably through unaided human judgment or purely technical means \cite{tolosana2020deepfakes, somoray2025deepfake}. Human performance varies substantially by task, medium, training, and contextual cues \cite{somoray2025deepfake}. This makes a governance strategy based solely on end-user vigilance unrealistic.

\subsection{Labels, warnings, and provenance}

A growing body of work has evaluated credibility labels and AI-generated-content labels. Aslett et al.\ found that news credibility labels had limited average effects on news-diet quality and did not reduce misperceptions overall, though some subgroup effects appeared \cite{aslett2022labels}. Li and Yang found that AIGC labels minimally affected perceived accuracy, message credibility, and sharing intention overall, although they did help users distinguish AI-generated from human-generated content \cite{li2024aigclabels}. Wittenberg et al.\ showed that labels clarifying how content was made and labels highlighting misleading potential can reduce belief in claims, but simple ``generated using AI'' labels have limited impact on engagement intentions \cite{wittenberg2025labeling}. Gamage et al.\ further showed that label design details---including iconography, wording, positioning, and information density---materially affect whether users trust the label \cite{gamage2025labeling}. 

These studies suggest a consistent pattern: visible labels matter, but \emph{label presence alone is not enough}. Labels need to be tied to a broader decision-support and governance flow. Provenance standards such as Content Credentials and the C2PA specification provide a promising substrate here. They define a cryptographically bound structure for recording an asset's provenance and are increasingly framed as a ``nutrition label'' for digital media \cite{c2pa2025spec, cai2026howitworks}. At the policy level, the EU AI Act established transparency obligations for certain AI-generated and AI-manipulated content, and the European Commission has since advanced a code of practice specifically on marking and labeling AI-generated content \cite{euai2024, eucommission2026}. These developments reinforce the normative direction toward disclosure, but they do not by themselves solve the user-experience problem of how disclosure should appear and how platforms should react after disclosure.

\subsection{Human-in-the-loop verification and community annotation}

Research on fact-checking consistently points toward hybrid systems rather than fully automated ones. Nakov et al.\ surveyed automated fact-checking as assistance for human fact-checkers rather than as a full replacement \cite{nakov2021factchecking}. Amershi et al.'s human--AI interaction guidelines similarly emphasize clear communication, reversible actions, and calibrated trust \cite{amershi2019guidelines}. Community-based fact-checking adds a second layer of human oversight. Drolsbach et al.\ found that community notes can increase trust in fact-checks relative to some expert-flag formats \cite{drolsbach2024community}. Slaughter et al.\ later showed that attached Community Notes reduced reposts, likes, replies, and views for false information online \cite{slaughter2025community}. 

These results support the idea that public notification by ordinary users should be treated as a first-class design component. A platform should not only tell users that content is AI-generated; it should also let users notify the platform and other viewers when they believe a post is synthetic or misleading, and it should attach that signal to downstream visibility and risk management.

\subsection{Feedback loops, judgment drift, and the case for circuit breakers}

The most important recent development for this paper is evidence that AI systems can reshape human judgment over time. Glickman and Sharot showed that human--AI interactions can amplify biases and internalize them in human decision-making, producing a snowball effect in which small AI errors become larger human biases \cite{glickman2025feedback}. This finding is highly relevant to social-media feeds, where repeated exposure rather than single-item persuasion often drives normalization. Spearing et al.\ similarly found that AI-generated misinformation can be persuasive and that multiple countermeasures may be needed to negate its effects \cite{spearing2025countering}. 

These results motivate a move from \emph{content-level} intervention to \emph{exposure-level} intervention. In other words, the platform should not wait for each item to be individually adjudicated before acting. It should monitor the cumulative risk that a user's feed is becoming oversaturated with synthetic, disputed, or high-risk content, and it should intervene when that risk exceeds a threshold. We call this intervention a \emph{circuit breaker}.

\section{Design Thesis and System Requirements}

TrustFeed is built around a normative and operational thesis: \textbf{users should never have to infer AI origin when the platform can disclose it, and platforms should never remain passive when cumulative synthetic exposure begins to erode judgment.} This thesis yields four system requirements.

\subsection{R1: Disclosure by default, not by exception}

Every public post should surface a visible origin state. The state should be informative rather than accusatory and should distinguish creation-process categories instead of collapsing them into a single binary. We propose the taxonomy in Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Default disclosure taxonomy for public digital content}
\label{tab:taxonomy}
\begin{tabular}{p{3.2cm}p{9.2cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{State} & \textbf{Meaning} \\
\midrule
AI-generated & Core expressive content was generated by an AI system. \\
AI-assisted & AI materially assisted creation or transformation, but human authorship remains substantial. \\
Human-declared & Uploader declares no material AI generation or transformation. \\
Provenance unavailable & The platform lacks reliable origin metadata or declaration. \\
Disputed & Community and system signals conflict strongly enough to require caution. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

This taxonomy is intentionally closer to origin labeling than to moderation labeling. It tells users \emph{how the content came into being}; it does not, by itself, assert truth, quality, or policy violation.

\subsection{R2: Public notification and contestability}

Disclosure must remain contestable. Users need a lightweight way to notify the platform that a post appears AI-generated or AI-manipulated even when the uploader did not disclose it. TrustFeed therefore includes a public ``Notify AI-generated'' action that complements creator declaration. This mirrors the logic of community notes and other citizen fact-checking systems: platform intelligence is improved when the public can attach structured signals to contested content \cite{drolsbach2024community, slaughter2025community}. 

\subsection{R3: Provenance should be surfaced, not buried}

Cryptographic provenance is valuable only if ordinary users can see and act on it. TrustFeed treats C2PA-style metadata and related provenance receipts as evidence sources, but collapses them into simple interface states: a visible label, a confidence summary, and a drill-down panel for users who want more detail \cite{c2pa2025spec, cai2026howitworks}. This design follows long-standing human-centered AI guidance to reveal relevant system information in understandable form and at the right time \cite{amershi2019guidelines}.

\subsection{R4: Platforms must manage cumulative exposure risk}

Because the harm of synthetic media is cumulative as well as item-specific, platforms need exposure-aware safeguards. TrustFeed therefore introduces a circuit breaker that activates when synthetic-content saturation, dispute intensity, and judgment drift rise above configurable thresholds. The intervention is designed to reduce passive scrolling and to insert reflective friction before repeated exposure can normalize suspect content \cite{glickman2025feedback}.

\section{TrustFeed Architecture}

\subsection{Current proof-of-concept artifact}

The repository-backed TrustFeed prototype is an Angular application with standalone components, TypeScript, SCSS, and Angular Signals. Its implemented features include an AI declaration toggle during post creation, visible AI badges on posts, community voting on whether content ``Looks AI'' or ``Looks Human,'' aggregated confidence labels such as ``Likely AI'' and ``Disputed,'' content filtering, and platform statistics \cite{trustfeedrepo}. These features establish the core interaction pattern: disclosure, community feedback, and lightweight authenticity signals embedded directly in the feed.

\subsection{From artifact to research architecture}

This paper treats the repository as the nucleus of a broader platform-safety architecture. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} is described textually because the current submission is artifact-centered rather than diagram-heavy. The architecture has five layers:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Origin capture layer}: creator declarations, platform-side generation flags, and machine-readable provenance metadata.
    \item \textbf{Public notification layer}: user reports, vote distributions, and dispute indicators.
    \item \textbf{Interpretation layer}: synthesis of origin evidence into a default visible state and confidence explanation.
    \item \textbf{Exposure analytics layer}: rolling measures of saturation, dispute, diversity, and judgment drift.
    \item \textbf{Intervention layer}: circuit-breaker responses, downranking, share friction, and escalation for high-risk topics.
\end{enumerate}

TrustFeed therefore shifts the unit of governance from the isolated post to the \emph{post-with-context}: its provenance, its contestation status, and its contribution to a user's cumulative exposure profile.

\subsection{UI model: disclosure like a market label, not a hidden forensic flag}

The food-label analogy is not cosmetic. In consumer markets, labels are expected to be fast, visible, and informative. Users do not inspect a molecular assay to learn whether a product is organic; they rely on a default disclosure channel that is legible at the point of decision. TrustFeed applies the same interaction logic to social media. Each post should display an origin ribbon or badge in the main viewport, not in a secondary settings panel. Users should also be able to open a deeper provenance card if they need more detail, including who declared the state, whether the state is backed by machine-readable provenance, and whether the public has challenged the declaration.

\subsection{Public notification flow}

When a viewer believes a post is AI-generated or AI-manipulated, the viewer can trigger ``Notify AI-generated.'' That action increments a structured signal rather than a free-form complaint count. Specifically, it contributes to:

\begin{itemize}
    \item a community suspicion score;
    \item a dispute ratio between creator declaration and community interpretation;
    \item a moderation queue for high-risk posts;
    \item training data for future interface and risk-model tuning.
\end{itemize}

The flow is contestable in both directions. Uploaders can revise their declaration; moderators or trusted reviewers can override it; and provenance evidence can raise or lower confidence. The goal is not to deputize the crowd as the final truth authority, but to make the public an active signal source in authenticity governance.

\section{Circuit Breaker Methodology}

\subsection{Why a circuit breaker is necessary}

Labeling each post independently does not address the systemic problem that users experience feeds as streams. A user who sees ten synthetic or disputed posts in a row is not facing ten isolated decisions; the user is entering a potentially distorting exposure regime. Because repeated AI interaction can alter human judgments and amplify bias \cite{glickman2025feedback}, platforms need a mechanism analogous to volatility pauses in financial markets: when the information stream becomes abnormally risky, the system should momentarily slow it down.

\subsection{Signal design}

For a user $u$ over a rolling window $w$ of recent impressions, TrustFeed computes the following normalized signals:

\begin{align}
A(u,w) &= \frac{\text{AI-labeled impressions}}{\text{total impressions}} \\
D(u,w) &= \frac{\text{disputed impressions}}{\text{total impressions}} \\
V(u,w) &= 1 - \frac{H(\text{source categories})}{\log K} \\
J(u,w) &= \left| \hat{p}_{u,w} - b_u \right| \\
P(u,w) &= \frac{\text{public-interest synthetic impressions}}{\text{total impressions}}
\end{align}

where $A$ is the AI saturation ratio, $D$ is the dispute ratio, $V$ is the inverse diversity score based on normalized entropy over source categories, $J$ is the judgment-drift term comparing current probe performance or judgment tendency $\hat{p}_{u,w}$ to the user's own recent baseline $b_u$, and $P$ measures the share of synthetic content touching public-interest topics such as elections, public health, identity-based claims, emergency events, or civic conflict.

The overall circuit-breaker risk is:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CBR}(u,w) = \alpha A(u,w) + \beta D(u,w) + \gamma V(u,w) + \delta J(u,w) + \epsilon P(u,w),
\end{equation}

with nonnegative weights $\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta,\epsilon$ set by policy and tuned empirically. This formulation is intentionally interpretable: each term corresponds to a risk that can be explained to users and auditors.

\subsection{Trigger tiers}

TrustFeed uses progressive intervention rather than a single hard stop. Table~\ref{tab:tiers} summarizes the tiers.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Proposed circuit-breaker intervention tiers}
\label{tab:tiers}
\begin{tabular}{p{2.1cm}p{3.1cm}p{7.4cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Tier} & \textbf{Trigger condition} & \textbf{Intervention} \\
\midrule
Tier 0 & $\mathrm{CBR} < \tau_1$ & No intervention beyond standard disclosure. \\
Tier 1 & $\tau_1 \le \mathrm{CBR} < \tau_2$ & Inline notice explaining elevated synthetic exposure; inject more human-declared and provenance-backed content. \\
Tier 2 & $\tau_2 \le \mathrm{CBR} < \tau_3$ & Share friction, slower feed refresh, and mandatory provenance card on disputed posts. \\
Tier 3 & $\mathrm{CBR} \ge \tau_3$ or repeated high-risk public-interest exposure & Full-screen pause, temporary suppression of recommender acceleration, escalation to trusted review for top disputed posts. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The design intentionally favors \emph{friction} over outright removal. Friction preserves user agency while interrupting passive consumption loops. In public-interest contexts, however, escalation and temporary throttling may be justified because the externality cost is higher.

\subsection{Judgment-drift measurement}

Judgment drift is the hardest signal because it refers to a change in the user's decision quality or tendency over time. TrustFeed proposes two mechanisms:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Micro-probes}: occasional low-burden authenticity probes embedded in the feed to estimate whether the user is over-accepting or over-rejecting synthetic content.
    \item \textbf{Behavioral baselines}: comparison of current classification tendencies with the user's own recent historical baseline, rather than with a population-wide norm.
\end{enumerate}

This design choice matters ethically. A circuit breaker should not punish users for disagreeing with majority opinion. It should activate when there is evidence that the user's own judgment process is becoming less calibrated under concentrated synthetic exposure.

\subsection{Why circuit breakers complement labels}

The empirical literature suggests that simple labels often improve recognition without strongly reducing engagement \cite{li2024aigclabels, wittenberg2025labeling}. A circuit breaker addresses that gap by shifting the intervention target. Instead of asking a label to both inform and de-amplify, the system assigns distinct responsibilities to distinct components:
\begin{itemize}
    \item labels disclose process;
    \item public notification adds contestability;
    \item provenance adds evidence;
    \item circuit breakers manage cumulative exposure and engagement risk.
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation Methodology}

Because the current TrustFeed repository is a proof-of-concept front-end artifact rather than a completed experimental platform, the appropriate empirical stance is a forward-looking evaluation design rather than a fabricated results section. We propose a mixed-method program with three hypotheses.

\subsection{Research hypotheses}

\textbf{H1.} Default AI-origin disclosure will improve users' ability to identify synthetic content relative to a no-label baseline.

\textbf{H2.} Disclosure combined with public notification and community verification will improve trust calibration more than disclosure alone.

\textbf{H3.} Disclosure combined with circuit breakers will reduce willingness to share disputed synthetic content under repeated-exposure conditions more than any label-only condition.

\subsection{Experimental conditions}

Participants would interact with a realistic feed simulator under four conditions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No label}: synthetic and non-synthetic content appear without origin disclosures.
    \item \textbf{Disclosure only}: posts carry default origin labels.
    \item \textbf{Disclosure + public notification}: posts carry origin labels and community-contestation signals.
    \item \textbf{Full TrustFeed}: origin labels, public notification, provenance drill-down, and circuit-breaker interventions.
\end{enumerate}

A repeated-exposure manipulation would vary the concentration of synthetic content and the share of disputed public-interest items in the feed.

\subsection{Outcome measures}

The primary outcome is \textbf{authenticity judgment accuracy}. Secondary outcomes include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{trust calibration}: alignment between user confidence and correctness;
    \item \textbf{sharing restraint}: self-reported and behavioral willingness to like, repost, or forward;
    \item \textbf{label comprehension}: whether users correctly interpret each disclosure state;
    \item \textbf{perceived agency}: whether users feel informed rather than manipulated by the intervention;
    \item \textbf{fatigue and reactance}: whether the system becomes annoying or is ignored over time.
\end{itemize}

The study should include qualitative interviews and diary components to understand how people interpret labels such as ``AI-assisted'' versus ``AI-generated,'' and whether the organic/non-organic analogy helps or hinders mental models.

\subsection{Why this evaluation design matters}

This evaluation design addresses three weaknesses in prior work. First, many label studies test isolated labels outside a realistic feed environment \cite{li2024aigclabels}. Second, engagement effects are often measured without cumulative-exposure manipulation \cite{wittenberg2025labeling}. Third, provenance systems are often studied as technical standards rather than public interfaces \cite{c2pa2025spec}. TrustFeed instead treats authenticity governance as a feed-level human-centered systems problem.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{From detection to accountability}

TrustFeed deliberately avoids framing authenticity as a contest between perfect AI detectors and perfect human perception. Both are limited \cite{somoray2025deepfake, kirchenbauer2023watermark}. The more durable objective is accountability: content should carry an origin state, the public should be able to contest that state, and the platform should slow down when exposure conditions become risky. This is a shift from \emph{forensic certainty} to \emph{governance sufficiency}.

\subsection{Disclosure is not censorship}

A recurring concern is that mandatory AI-origin disclosure may stigmatize legitimate creative or assistive AI use. TrustFeed addresses this by separating disclosure from moral judgment. A visible ``AI-assisted'' label does not imply low quality, misinformation, or policy violation. It is analogous to market disclosure: users get to know what they are consuming and then decide what to do with that information. This process-based framing aligns better with the empirical literature showing that labels are most useful as informational devices when they are clear and well-designed \cite{gamage2025labeling, wittenberg2025labeling}.

\subsection{Public notification as democratic platform sensing}

Allowing the public to notify that content is AI-generated operationalizes a minimal form of participatory governance. It does not replace professional review or cryptographic provenance. Instead, it creates an additional sensing channel that can surface failures in creator disclosure, reveal patterns the platform missed, and improve user trust when paired with transparent explanation \cite{drolsbach2024community, slaughter2025community}. The design challenge is to prevent brigading and low-quality reports, which is why public notification must be aggregated, reputation-aware, and contestable.

\subsection{The circuit breaker as a platform-safety primitive}

The strongest conceptual contribution of this paper is the circuit breaker. Social platforms already optimize dynamically for watch time, clicks, and relevance. There is no principled reason they cannot also optimize for authenticity safety. A circuit breaker is a modest but powerful expression of that idea: when the platform detects that a user is entering a regime of unusually high synthetic saturation and contestation, it temporarily privileges reflection over speed.

\section{Limitations}

This paper has four limitations. First, the current TrustFeed repository implements disclosure and community signaling, but not the full provenance and circuit-breaker stack; those components are specified here as an extension of the artifact rather than a completed deployment \cite{trustfeedrepo}. Second, the paper is a design-and-methodology contribution, not an empirical validation paper, and therefore does not claim finished user-study results. Third, provenance infrastructure remains uneven across tools and platforms, so some content will necessarily remain in a provenance-unavailable state even under a better design regime \cite{c2pa2025spec}. Fourth, any circuit-breaker system introduces normative choices about thresholds, public-interest categories, and intervention severity; those choices require transparent governance and iterative auditing.

\section{Conclusion}

The synthetic-media era requires more than forensic detectors and sporadic labels. It requires a new default for public digital content: visible AI-origin disclosure, contestable community notification, and exposure-aware platform safeguards. TrustFeed advances this agenda by arguing that disclosure should be treated like origin labeling in mature consumer systems---always present, easy to understand, and separate from downstream quality judgments. Building on a proof-of-concept repository that already supports creator declaration, AI badges, and community voting, this paper develops a broader architecture in which provenance is surfaced, the public can notify suspected AI content, and a circuit breaker intervenes when synthetic exposure becomes risky. 

The result is a human-centered authenticity governance model for social platforms. Its aim is not to eliminate AI content or to promise perfect detection. Its aim is to preserve human judgment under new informational conditions by making origin visible, contestation actionable, and amplification interruptible.
